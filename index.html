Overview

Welcome to Ischemic Stroke Lesion Segmentation (ISLES), a medical image segmentation challenge at the International Conference on Medical Image Computing and Computer Assisted Intervention (MICCAI) 2015. We aim to provide a platform for a fair and direct comparison of methods for ischemic stroke lesion segmentation from multi-spectral MRI images. A public dataset of diverse ischemic stroke cases and a suitable automatic evaluation procedure will be made available for the two following tasks:

SISS: sub-acute ischemic stroke lesion segmentation

SPES: perfusion-diffusion mismatch for stroke penumbra estimation

About

Why challenges?

Medical image processing comprises a large number of tasks, for which new methods are regulary proposed. But, more often than not, it is difficult to to compare their fitness, as the reported results are obtained on private dataset. A challenge aims to overcome these shortcomings by providing (1) a public dataset that reflects the diversity of the problem and (2) a platform for a fair and direct comparison of methods with suitable evaluation measures. Thus, the scientific progress is promoted. (see here for a detailed rationale behind challenges).

Past challenges

During recent years, the need for challenges has been emphasized in the medical image processing community and especially in the scope of the International Conference on Medical Image Computing and Computer Assisted Intervention (MICCAI) and the International Symposium on Biomedical Imaging (ISBI) conferences, a number of challenges have been successfully organized. All of these are collect centrally at grand-challenges.org.

The ISLES challenge

With ISLES, we provide such a challenge covering ischemic stroke lesion segmentation in multi-spectral MRI data. The task is backed by a well established clinical and research Motivation and a large number of already existing methods. Each team will be free to participate in either one or both of two sub-tasks: sub-acute ischemic stroke lesion segmentation (SISS) and perfusion-diffusion mismatch for stroke penumbra estimation (SPES).

SISS: Automatic segmentation of ischemic stroke lesion volumes from multi-spectral MRI sequences acquired in the sub-acute stroke development stage.

How it works

Interested participants can download a set of training cases with associated expert segmentations of the stroke lesions to train and evaluate their approach. Shortly before the MICCAI takes place, a set of test cases will be made available and the participants upload their segmentation results as well as a shot-paper describing their method. The organizers then evaluate each case and establish a ranking of the participating teams. At the MICCAI conference, the results will be presented in the scope of a workshop and the results discussed together with invited experts. Each team presents their method as a poster and, Furthermore, a number of selected teams will have the opportunity to hold a small presentation detailing their method. After the challenge, the organizers and participants will together compose a high-impact journal paper to publish their findings.

Clinical motivation

A stroke or colloquially brain attack is a sudden occurrence of brain disease, which often involves the loss of functions of the central nervous system and is caused by disturbance in the blood supply of the brain. Strokes can be classified into the groups ischemic, caused by undersupply of blood in a brain area, and hemorrhagic, caused by undersupply and pressure of leaking blood in a brain area due to a broken vessel.

Fig. 1: Defect of the blood-brain barrier after stroke shown in MRI. T1-weighted images, left image without right image with contrast medium administration.

Research motivation

These lesions can be identified (segmented) in multimodal MRI imaging data. With this challenge, we aim to take a step forward to computer-aided methods for automatic segmentation of brain lesions. One clinical application could be the better understanding of specific brain area functions by connecting the caused dysfunction with the affected brain area. The required processes would be strongly simplified by automatic segmentation algorithms.

Rules

What is required to participate?

Each team wishing to participate in the ISLES challenge is required to:

  1. register at the data distribution and evaluation platform (see registration for more details).
  2. upload the test data segmentation results before the deadline (see Dates)
  3. submit a short paper describing their method and their results on the training data set (see Short Paper)
  4. register to the associated MICCAI workshop and pay the attendance fee (possible right up until the day of the challenge)
  5. be present at the workshop with at least one team member

Which methods are called for?

All types of automatic stroke lesion segmentation methods are welcome. We specifically do not restrict the participation to new, innovative and unpublished methods, but invite research groups from all over the world to enter their existing method in the competition. Semi-automatic methods are eligible for participation and will appear in the ranking, but out of competition, as it is impossible to rate the influence of the manual steps in a fair manner.

Participating in one or both of the sub-task (SISS & SPES)

We encourage all teams to apply their method to both of the sub-tasks to highlight its generalization ability. But it is equally possible to submit a specialized approach for each of the task or just to ne of them.

A number of special rules applies to simplify the participation in multiple of these events.

Participation in the Brain Tumor Image Segmentation (BRATS) challenge

The organizers of ISLES and BRATS took care to apply the same pre-processing steps to their data, to enable the participants of one of the challenges to furthermore participate in the respective other. Especially machine learning based approaches should be easy to adapt. Double contributions are highly welcome and only one registration at the MICCAI workshop is required.

Data usage of ISLES and BRATS for the Brain Lesion Segmentation (BrainLeS) workshop

Participants are free to use the training data from both, ISLES and BRATS, to evaluate methods submitted to BrainLeS, but are then encouraged to also participate in the respective challenge. In such case, they are not required to submit the short-paper to ISLES respectively BRATS, instead their (accepted) BrainLeS manuscript will suffice, as long as it states the results obtained on the training set.

Short paper

ISLES requires the participating teams to submit a short-paper of 4 pages in LNCS format, that will be reviewed by the organizers. This paper will be distributed among the MICCAI attendees on the MICCAI USB drive and uploaded to the challenge result web-page. See Dates for the associated deadlines. In case the BrainLeS workshop results in a special issue, some selected methods from the BRATS and ISLES challenge will be called upon to contribute an article.

Submitting results for multiple methods

A team can submit results from multiple methods, but an extra registration to the MICCAI challenge is required for each additionally submitted result.

Privacy and data copyright

By registering, each team agrees to use the provided data only in the scope of the workshop and neither pass it on to a third party nor use it for other publications. After the workshop took place, the data will be released under a research license.

    The evaluation comprises three evaluation measures, each of them highlighting different aspects of the segmentation quality. From the computed evaluation scores, the global rating of each team is established as described below.

    Expert segmentations

    All expert segmentation were prepared by experience raters. For SISS, two sets will be made available, for SPES one.

    Evaluation measures

    Three principal evaluation measures are employed to evaluate the quality of a segmentation compared to the reference ground-truth: The Dice's coefficient (DC, see [wikiDC]) denotes the volume overlap, the average symmetric surface distance (ASSD) the surface fit and the Hausdorff distance (HD, see [wikiHD]) the maximum error. Furthermore, precision & recall (see [wikiPreAndRec]) are provided to reveal over- resp. under-segmentation, but won't be used in the computation of the team ranks, as they are indirectly reflected by the DC value.

    Dice's coefficient (DC)

    The DC measures the similarity between two datasets. Considering two sets of volume voxels \(A\) and \(B\), the \(DC\) value is given as:

    $$DC=\dfrac{2|A\cap B|}{|A|+|B|}$$

    A value of 0 indicates no overlap, a value of 1 perfect similarity. During interpretation, it should be kept in mind that the \(DC\) is known to yield higher values for larger volumes i.e. a \(DC\) of 0.9 for lung segmentation is considered average, as is a \(DC\) of 0.60 for MS lesions.

    Fig.: The two sets \(A\), \(B\) and their union.

    Note that the \(DC\) is the same as the F1-score, monotonic in Jaccard simmilarity and the harmonic mean of precision & recall. See here for more details.

    Average symmetric surface distance (ASSD)

    The \(ASSD\) denotes the average distance between the volumes surface points averaged over both directions. Considering two sets of surface points \(A\) and \(B\), the average surface distance (\(ASD\)) is given as:

    $$ASD(A,B) = \frac{\sum_{a\in A}min_{b\in B}d(a,b)}{|A|}$$

    with \(d(a,b)\) being the Euclidean distance between the points \(a\) and \(b\). Since \(ASD(A,B)\neq ASD(B,A)\), the \(ASSD\) is then given as:

    $$ASSD(A,B) = \frac{ASD(A,B)+ASD(B,A)}{2}$$

    It is given in \(mm\), the lower the better, and works equally well for large and small objects.

    Hausdorff distance (HD)

    The \(HD\) denotes the maximum distance between two volumes surface points and hence denotes outliers, especially when multiple objects are considered. It is defined as:

    $$HD(A,B)=\text{max}\{\max_{a\in A}\min_{b\in B}d(a,b),\max_{b\in B}\min_{a\in A}d(b,a)\}$$

    Precision & Recall

    The \(precision\) (also called positive predictive value) and \(recall\) (also known as sensitivity) of two sets can be defined as

    $$precision=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FP}$$

    and

    $$recall=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FN},$$

    where \(TP\) (true positive) are the overlapping points, \(FP\) (false positive) are the wrong segmented points in the segmentation and \(FN\) (false negative) are the not segmented points in the reference.

    Both measure take values in the range of \([0,1]\). A relatively high precision compared to the recall reveals under-segmentation and vice-versa, as depicted in the following figure:

    Ranking

    To establish a teams rank, the following steps are performed:

    1. Compute the DC, ASSD & HD values for each case
    2. Establish each teams rank for DC, ASSD & HD separately for each dataset
    3. Compute the mean rank over all three evaluation measures per case to obtain the teams rank for the case
    4. Compute the mean over all case-specific ranks to obtain the teams final rank

    Very easy or difficult cases

    By not averaging the actual evaluation measures, but rather the ranks in each, the system is not affected by very difficult or easy cases: If all methods return low results for one case, the small difference will still count in the ranking and the overall impression will not be distorted.

    Missing or not processable cases

    If a case has not been uploaded or could not be processed for some reason, the associated team will be assigned the last place for this case.

    Complete mismatch

    A complete mismatch (i.e. a DC value of 0) will be treated as a missing case as detailed above (and the obtained ASSD & HD values ignored).

    Shared ranks

    Multiple teams can share a single rank. This will then be always the highest, e.g.:
